Abstract 

Large vision-language models (LVLMs) hallucinate: they as-sert visual details that the image does not support. A prin-cipled remedy is selective prediction with a distribution-free guarantee—verify each claim and abstain when the claim is not grounded, so that the hallucination rate among asserted claims is provably bounded. We show, however, that this guar-antee is bought at a brutal price: to keep the hallucination rate below 5% on a balanced object-existence benchmark, a state-of-the-art conformal filter must abstain on more than 80% of claims. We argue that abstention is wasteful when more visual evidence is cheaply available, and introduce Budgeted Con-formal Evidence Acquisition (BCEA) , which replaces the bi-nary answer/abstain decision with a three-way choice: answer, abstain, or acquire additional visual evidence by re-examining the image (zooming, cropping, or applying a claim-specific in-tervention) under a bounded compute budget. We make two observations. First, acquisition that is plugged naively into a calibrated filter breaks the statistical guarantee—realized risk overshoots the target by up to 17 points—because the acqui-sition step destroys the exchangeability that conformal cali-bration relies on. Second, folding the entire acquisition policy into the score function and re-calibrating on post-acquisition scores restores the finite-sample guarantee while still recov-ering coverage. BCEA further uses structured, claim-type-specific interventions; e.g., a single horizontal-flip interven-tion lifts spatial-relation verification from chance ( 0.57 AU-ROC) to 0.77 , where the global ungrounding score used by prior work is useless. Across the POPE benchmark and COCO-constructed existence and spatial-relation claims, on four open VLMs (LLaVA-1.5, Qwen2.5-VL, LLaVA-NeXT, InternVL2), BCEA controls the hallucination rate at the target level and consistently improves coverage over a guaranteed-abstention baseline. 

## 1 Introduction 

Large vision-language models (LVLMs) produce fluent, helpful descriptions of images, but they also hallucinate :they assert objects, attributes, and relations that are not present in the visual input (Leng et al. 2024; Huang et al. 2024; Guan et al. 2024). For deployment in settings that de-mand reliability—medical, scientific, assistive—fluency is not enough; what is needed is a guarantee that, whatever the model says, the rate of unsupported claims stays below a user-specified level. Conformal prediction offers exactly this kind of guarantee, and a recent line of work brings it to LVLMs. The strongest instance, ConfLVLM (Li et al. 2025), treats each generated detail as a hypothesis, scores it with a cheap uncertainty heuristic, and filters out claims whose score falls below a conformally calibrated threshold; the result is a finite-sample, distribution-free bound on the factual error rate of what re-mains. This is principled and attractive. But it inherits the central weakness of selective prediction: to be safe, it ab-stains . In our experiments, guaranteeing a hallucination rate below 5% forces such a filter to abstain on more than 80% of all claims (Figure 1). A reliable model that refuses four out of five questions is of limited use. We start from a simple observation: abstention is wasteful when more evidence is cheaply available. When a person is unsure whether a small object is present, they look closer; they do not give up. LVLMs admit the analogous move— re-encoding a zoomed crop, or re-scoring the claim under a targeted visual intervention—at the cost of a few extra forward passes. The question is whether this “look again” step can be added without forfeiting the statistical guarantee 

that motivated selective prediction in the first place. Two existing literatures each solve half of this problem. Conformal LVLM filters (Li et al. 2025; Tayebati et al. 2025) provide the guarantee but never acquire evidence—they only drop claims. Evidence-acquisition methods such as ReCoV-ERR (Srinivasan et al. 2024) do gather more evidence to rescue abstained questions, but they rely on heuristic risk-tolerance thresholds and provide no distribution-free guaran-tee; moreover they acquire textual evidence (LLM-generated follow-up questions) rather than re-examining the pixels. No method does both—acquire visual evidence and retain the guarantee—and, as we show, naively combining the two is not safe. We introduce Budgeted Conformal Evidence Acquisi-tion (BCEA) . BCEA replaces the binary answer/abstain decision with a three-way action—answer, abstain, or ac-quire —and makes three contributions. 

(1) Acquisition that is naive breaks the guarantee. Cal-ibrating a conformal threshold on a claim’s base score and then, at test time, replacing that score with a higher post-acquisition score is the obvious thing to do, and it is wrong. Finally, we give a tight characterization of when acquisi-tion helps: guaranteed coverage at a fixed risk is a single point on the score’s ROC curve, so acquisition raises coverage at every risk level if and only if it improves the ROC (The-orem 2)—a measurable criterion that explains why model-guided crops beat a uniform grid and why per-claim budget allocation, by contrast, does not help. We validate all of this on object-existence and spatial-relation claims from COCO and the POPE benchmark, across three open VLMs, with head-to-head baselines. 

## 2 Related Work 

Hallucination in LVLMs. Object hallucination is mea-sured by benchmarks such as POPE (Li et al. 2023), AM-BER, and, for free-form generation, THRONE (Kaul et al. 2024); HallusionBench (Guan et al. 2024) separates lan-guage hallucination from visual illusion, and TUBench (He et al. 2024) probes unanswerable questions. Mitigation meth-ods are largely heuristic: contrastive decoding (VCD (Leng et al. 2024), HALC), over-trust penalties (OPERA (Huang et al. 2024)), and preference optimization (RLHF-V, V-DPO). None provide a statistical guarantee on the residual halluci-nation rate. 

Conformal and selective prediction for generation. Con-formal factuality (Mohri and Hashimoto 2024) and confor-mal abstention for LLMs (Yadkori et al. 2024) bound the error rate of text generation by filtering or backing off claims. For LVLMs, ConfLVLM (Li et al. 2025) provides finite-sample factuality guarantees by hypothesis-testing each claim with global uncertainty heuristics, and CAP (Tayebati et al. 2025) learns adaptive conformal abstention thresholds for LLM-s/VLMs. All of these only drop claims. We add an acquisi-tion action and show the resulting validity subtlety and its fix. 

Evidence acquisition and active perception. ReCoV-ERR (Srinivasan et al. 2024) reduces over-abstention by col-lecting LLM-generated follow-up QA as evidence, under a heuristic risk tolerance and without distribution-free guar-antees. Zoom/crop methods such as ViCrop (Zhang et al. 2023) and CropVLM improve fine-grained accuracy but tar-get accuracy, not guaranteed risk; our CLIP-guided acqui-sition adopts the same zoom idea as a verification tool and adds the guarantee. 

Conformal validity under adaptivity. That applying a fixed, label-independent transformation to both calibration and test points preserves exchangeability is known: test-time augmentation can be folded into a conformal predictor with-out losing coverage (Shanmugam et al. 2025), and conformal risk control has been combined with instance-level stopping under a compute budget for LLM reasoning (Wang et al. 2026) and with joint calibration–test thresholds for selective risk (Xu, Guo, and Wei 2025). Our theory is an instance of this principle; our contribution is to (i) identify that visual 

evidence acquisition silently violates the guarantee when this principle is ignored—the natural and tempting deployment— and (ii) bring acquisition into the pixels via structured, claim-type interventions. To our knowledge BCEA is the first to couple guaranteed VLM hallucination filtering with visual evidence acquisition. 

## 3 Preliminaries 

Claim-level selective prediction. A response is decom-posed into atomic claims c1, . . . , c m (Li et al. 2025). Each claim c about image x has an unknown binary label y ∈{0, 1} (1 = supported by the image). A scoring function 

s(x, c ) ∈ R ranks claims by how grounded they appear (higher = more grounded). A selective rule asserts c iff s(x, c ) ≥ τ and otherwise abstains. We measure coverage = Pr[ s ≥ τ ], risk = Pr[ y = 0 | s ≥ τ ], (1) i.e. the fraction of claims asserted and the hallucination rate among asserted claims. 

Split-conformal selective risk control. Given a calibra-tion set of i.i.d. (or exchangeable) labeled claims and a target level α, we choose the smallest threshold τ (maximizing cov-erage) such that an upper confidence bound on the calibration risk is at most α. For accepted calibration claims with k er-rors out of n, we use the exact Clopper-Pearson upper bound 

Rδ (k, n ) = Beta −11−δ (k + 1 , n − k) and scan τ from most to least conservative, stopping at the first τ with Rδ ≤ α (a fixed-sequence procedure (Angelopoulos et al. 2025)). On exchangeable test claims this controls the selective risk at level α with probability 1 − δ.

Why we do not claim budget allocation. A natural ex-tension is to spend a fixed total budget unevenly across claims; under diminishing returns the optimum is greedy water-filling on marginal gains (a standard exchange argu-ment, App.). Empirically this gives no improvement over uni-form allocation on existence claims ( ≤ 0.4 coverage points, within CI; App.). The reason is structural: with a single global threshold, an extra look raises the scores of true and false boundary claims alike, and at allocation time their la-bels are unknown—so any label-agnostic allocation is, to first order, coverage-neutral. By Theorem 2, allocation can help only by making the aggregate score more informative, which requires heterogeneous and predictable per-claim in-formativeness; absent that, uniform is optimal. We therefore do not claim allocation as a contribution—a negative result we think is clarifying. 

## 5 Experiments 

Setup. We use LLaVA-1.5-7B (Liu et al. 2024) (main) and Qwen2.5-VL-7B (Bai et al. 2025) (generality), and construct claims from COCO val2017 ground-truth instance annota-tions (no extra labeling). Existence claims pair each image with present objects (true) and sampled absent objects (false); 

spatial claims pair the two largest, horizontally separated ob-jects as “ A is to the left/right of B”, with the swapped direction as the false claim. Each claim score is one forward pass; ac-quisition adds B=5 grid-crop passes. Conformal calibration uses a 50 /50 split averaged over 300 random splits, δ=0 .1;we report test coverage and the 90 th-percentile realized risk. 

5.1 Guaranteed Abstention Is Conservative 

Figure 1 shows the risk–coverage frontier on 1,440 balanced existence claims. To certify a hallucination rate ≤ 5% , the global-score filter can assert only 18% of claims; even at a lenient ≤ 20% it asserts 40% . This is the cost of a guaran-tee without acquisition, and the motivation for BCEA. The evidence-sufficiency score sglob already separates grounded from hallucinated claims (AUROC 0.838 ) far better than raw claim likelihood ( 0.808 ), confirming that image-dependence is the right signal; acquisition raises it to 0.879 .

5.2 Evidence Is Localized 

Before using acquisition we verify that the grounding signal is spatially localized. On 800 true existence claims, mask-ing the object’s ground-truth region lowers the claim log-likelihood by 0.109 on average, whereas masking an equal-area irrelevant region raises it slightly ( −0.050 ); the paired difference is +0 .159 with a 67% win rate ( p ≪ 10 −10 ). Figure 4 makes this visual: sliding an occlusion patch and recording the drop in claim likelihood produces a heatmap that concentrates on the object. The model’s confidence in a true claim depends specifically on the pixels that support it—the premise behind region- and intervention-based scor-ing.

Figure 6 sweeps the acquisition budget B (extra views per claim), re-calibrating at each B so the guarantee al-ways holds. 5.6 Model-Guided Acquisition 

A uniform crop grid is blind to where the object is. We in-stead let CLIP propose crops: among a multi-scale grid of candidate windows we keep the five whose CLIP embed-ding is most similar to “a photo of a ⟨object ⟩” (Figure 8), using no ground-truth location. Table 4 compares no ac-quisition, the uniform grid, and CLIP-guided crops on the same claims. Guided acquisition raises detection AUROC (0.824 → 0.862 → 0.882 ) and guaranteed coverage at every level (e.g. 0.28 → 0.33 → 0.37 at α=0 .10 ). The mechanism is exactly the one the grid missed: guided crops nearly double 

the acquisition gain on small objects ( 0.25 → 0.43 , Fig-ure 9), which a fixed grid tends to crop away, while leaving large-object gains unchanged. Allocating a fixed total bud-get greedily by marginal gain rather than uniformly makes no measurable difference here ( ≤ 0.2 coverage points), con-sistent with the roughly homogeneous per-claim gains on existence claims; we expect allocation to matter when claim difficulty is heterogeneous.

## 6 Discussion and Limitations 

Our study uses four backbones (LLaVA-1.5, LLaVA-NeXT, Qwen2.5-VL, InternVL2) and two working claim families (existence, left/right); building a principled per-claim-type intervention library is the natural next step. The method also has a clear scope boundary: evidence sufficiency can only certify what the backbone can perceive. Counting, relative size, above/below, color attributes, and color–object bind-ing are all near chance for these backbones ( sglob AUROC 

0.50 –0.58 ), and no intervention we tried (instance masking, vertical flip, grayscaling) recovers a usable signal, so cover-age collapses to zero on those families. The binding result is telling: our score cannot certify whether “the red cup ” is correctly bound precisely because the backbone itself does not reliably make that distinction. This is the faithful be-havior: where a 7–8B VLM genuinely cannot perceive a property, our scores report ungrounded rather than fabri-cate confidence, and the guaranteed filter abstains. ## B Implementation Details 

Models. LLaVA-1.5-7B and Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct (HuggingFace checkpoints), fp16, single A6000 each; in-ference only, no fine-tuning. 

Scoring. For claim c and visual input v we feed the chat prompt “USER: <image> What is in this image? The blank reference ∅ is an all-black image of the same size; crops are five overlapping 62% windows (four corners + center); region masks and one-instance masks black out ground-truth COCO boxes; the flip intervention is a horizontal mirror.
