## VoidPadding: Let [VOID] Handle Padding in Masked Diffusion Language Models so that [EOS] Can Focus on Semantic Termination 

Chunyu Liu 1, Zhengyang Fan 1, Kaisen Yang 1, Alex Lamb 1*1Tsinghua University 

> {liucy24,fzy24,yks23}@mails.tsinghua.edu.cn lambalex@tsinghua.edu.cn

Abstract 

MDLMs generate text by denoising a pre-allocated masked response canvas, making response-length modeling central to instruc-tion tuning. Existing MDLMs often inherit the autoregressive convention of using repeated 

[EOS] tokens for padding during instruction tuning, giving [EOS] a dual role as both a se-mantic terminator and a padding token. We show that this dual role is a root cause of [EOS] 

overflow under large-block decoding. To de-couple these roles, we propose VoidPadding, which introduces [VOID] for padding and re-serves [EOS] for termination. During inference, the learned [EOS] signal enables early stopping, while the learned [VOID] signal guides adap-tive response canvas expansion. On Dream-7B-Instruct, VoidPadding improves the block-size-averaged four-task mean across mathemat-ical reasoning and code generation benchmarks by +17 .84 points over the original model and 

+6 .95 points over RainbowPadding, while re-ducing decoding NFE by 55.7% on average. Code is available at https://github.com/ Haru-LCY/VoidPadding .

1 Introduction 

Masked diffusion language models (Austin et al., 2021a; Wang et al., 2025; Lou et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2025) (MDLMs) offer a promising alternative to autoregressive language models (Radford et al., 2018) (ARLMs). Whereas ARLMs generate tokens sequentially from left to right, MDLMs use bidi-rectional attention to denoise the entire sequence in parallel (Bie et al., 2026). This bidirectional condi-tioning, however, makes MDLMs sensitive to the padding tokens introduced in instruction tuning. During instruction tuning (Chung et al., 2024), instruction–response pairs are typically processed in batches for efficiency and padded to the maxi-mum sequence length within each mini-batch. Fol-

> *Corresponding author.

lowing a convention inherited from ARLM train-ing, MDLMs often use repeated [EOS] tokens as padding (Nie et al., 2026). In ARLMs, causal mask-ing (Vaswani et al., 2017) prevents earlier positions from conditioning on [EOS] padding, so [EOS] pri-marily serves as a semantic terminator. In MDLMs, however, [EOS] padding is also included in the training target. Thus, [EOS] takes on two roles: the semantic terminator role and the padding role. During inference, MDLMs generate on a prede-fined response canvas whose positions are jointly modeled under bidirectional attention (Cheng et al., 2025). The padding role of [EOS] introduced dur-ing instruction tuning therefore offers a length sig-nal: high [EOS] confidence near the tail indicates that the current canvas may exceed the desired out-put length (Li et al., 2025a; Yang et al., 2026). However, this signal becomes fragile under large-block decoding. Once high-confidence tail [EOS] 

tokens are generated, they become part of the de-noising context and can further increase nearby 

[EOS] probabilities, leading to short effective re-sponses and repeated [EOS] . This failure mode is known as [EOS] overflow (Kim et al., 2025a). Based on these insights, we propose Void-Padding , a lightweight training–inference co-design that introduces [VOID] as a new padding token and reserves [EOS] solely for semantic ter-mination. Training sequences are formatted as 

[prompt] [response] [EOS] [VOID]* , where each example contains a single [EOS] followed by 

[VOID] padding. During inference, [VOID] gener-ation is banned. We introduce [EOS] Termination ,which stops decoding once no masks remain before the leftmost visible [EOS] . This prevents [EOS] 

overflow and avoids redundant generation after the effective response. Additionally, we propose an optional inference-time VoidExpansion strategy, which uses the [VOID] length signal to expand in-sufficient canvases before denoising. Our contributions are:

2.1 Masked Diffusion Modeling 

MDLMs model generation as a denoising process over discrete sequences (Sahoo et al., 2024). Let 

V = {1, . . . , V } be the token vocabulary, M /∈ V 

the mask token, and zt ∈ (V ∪ { M})L the se-quence state at diffusion step t ∈ {0, . . . , T },where T is the total number of denoising steps. Here, t = 0 denotes clean data and t = T the fully masked sequence. The masked and visible positions are denoted by Mt = {i : zit = M} and 

¯Mt = {1, . . . , L } \ M t, respectively. The forward process gradually corrupts a clean sequence z0 ∼ pdata by independently masking each position over T steps. At each step, tokens are progressively replaced by M according to a fixed schedule, so that zT is fully masked. During training, a timestep t is sampled and the model predicts the original token at each masked posi-tion, yielding the denoising cross-entropy objective

MDLM decoding procedures include block dif-fusion (Arriola et al., 2025) and pure diffusion (Ye et al., 2025). Both of them start from a fully masked canvas of length L with block size B, where pure diffusion corresponds to B = L. At each step, the model uses bidirectional attention to condition on the whole sequence and predicts pθ(· | zt) for all i ∈ M t in parallel, so one denoising step cor-responds to one model forward pass. Decoding then proceeds in two steps within the current block. First, it selects commitment positions, commonly using confidence (Cai and Li, 2026) or negative entropy scores (Ben-Hamu et al., 2026). Second, it selects tokens for these positions, typically by argmax under pθ. Decoding moves to the next block once the current block is fully committed and terminates when all masks have been filled. 

2.2 Instruction-Tuning Data Construction 

Instruction tuning (IT) trains pretrained language models on prompt–response pairs, improving instruction following and zero-shot generaliza-tion (Chung et al., 2024). For efficient batch-ing, prompt–response sequences are terminated by 

[EOS] and padded to a common length. MDLM IT often follows the ARLM convention of us-ing [EOS] as the padding token. Let B =

{(pk, r k)}bk=1 be a mini-batch of size b, where pk is the prompt of length Pk and rk is the raw response of length ℓk. Over the IT data distribution, let ℓ

denote the raw response length before the terminal 

[EOS] , and define its CDF as F (m) = Pr( ℓ ≤ m).After appending one semantic terminator [EOS] ,each full sequence is padded with [EOS] to the maximum full-sequence length in the mini-batch, 

SB = max 1≤k≤b(Pk + ℓk + 1) . Thus, exam-ple k has padded response yk = rk ⊕ [EOS] ⊕

[EOS] LB,k −ℓk −1 of length LB,k = SB − Pk. Dur-ing IT, both noise and loss are applied only to yk.

3 Analysis of [EOS] Overflow 

[EOS] overflow is a common issue of MDLM infer-ence under large block sizes (Kim et al., 2025a). In a typical case, the model produces a very short an-swer followed by repeated [EOS] tokens, as shown in Figure 3(a). However, [EOS] overflow is not ob-served in ARLMs, even though both ARLMs and MDLMs use [EOS] as padding tokens during IT. This motivates us to ask: Why is [EOS] padding safe in ARLMs but fragile in MDLMs? 

Observation 1: [EOS] overflow is not caused by limited large-block generation capacity. 

We first test whether [EOS] overflow is caused by insufficient large-block generation capac-ity. Specifically, we ban [EOS] commitment in LLaDA-8B-Instruct by setting the [EOS] logit to 

−∞ throughout decoding, and then evaluate on GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) with L = B = 512 .Although this removes the semantic stopping sig-nal and often causes repeated reasoning traces and answers, it raises the accuracy of the first extracted answer from 28.43% to 70.66%. Thus, the model is not inherently unable to generate large blocks. 

Observation 2: Training with [EOS] padding teaches [EOS] to act as a length signal.

4 Method 

Motivated by this training–inference role mismatch, we propose VoidPadding , which introduces a new 

[VOID] token as the padding token, so that [EOS] 

serves only as the semantic terminator. We fur-ther design VoidExpansion for inference, which uses the [VOID] length signal learned during IT to expand an insufficient canvas before decoding. To-gether, this training–inference co-design decouples padding from semantic termination while preserv-ing the hidden length signal carried by [VOID] to-kens. Figure 1 provides an overview of the method, with details given in the following subsections. 

4.1 VoidPadding Instruction-Tuning Design. VoidPadding follows the IT data construction described in Section 2.2, except that padding [EOS] tokens are replaced by 

[VOID] tokens. Thus, for B = {(pk, r k)}bk=1 and

. Notably, we apply standard MDLM masking for non-[EOS] po-sitions. The single [EOS] token in each example is always masked, forcing the model to predict it as a stable semantic terminator. 

Inference Design. During inference, [VOID] re-mains a hidden padding signal and is never gen-erated: setting its logit to −∞ before token se-lection prevents the high-confidence [VOID] tail from becoming visible output. We also propose 

[EOS] Termination , which stops decoding once all positions before the leftmost visible [EOS] are un-masked and then discards the rest of the canvas. In contrast, VanillaStopping , the standard MDLM stopping rule, stops only after all masked positions are decoded (Section 2.1), causing redundant de-coding after the semantic endpoint.

5 Experiments 

Setup. Our main experiments use two instruction-tuned MDLMs: LLaDA-8B-Instruct (hereafter LLaDA) (Nie et al., 2026) and Dream-7B-Instruct (hereafter Dream) (Ye et al., 2025). Quality met-rics are accuracy on GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) and MATH500 (Hendrycks et al., 2021), and pass@1 on HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021) and MBPP (Austin et al., 2021b). The main efficiency metric is the average number of forward passes per example (hereafter NFE). In all tables, bold marks the best result and underline marks the second best within each comparison. For Dream, we use a decoding wrapper to enable the same block-wise decoding protocol as LLaDA; details are provided in Appendix C. We compare against the original models and RainbowPadding (Kim et al., 2025a), which aims to avoid [EOS] overflow by cyclically using 7 distinct padding tokens. On both LLaDA and Dream, RainbowPadding and VoidPadding use LoRA SFT (Hu et al., 2022) on the same 0.5M-example data and identical training configurations, except that VoidPadding uses 67% of Rainbow-Padding’s training steps. Detailed training configu-ration is provided in Appendix A. 

5.1 Fixed-Length Block-Size Robustness 

We first evaluate fixed-length robustness with gen-eration length L = 512 and a maximum denoising-step budget T = 512 , while varying the block size 

B. Table 1 reports B ∈ { 64 , 128 , 512 }. Complete block-size results are in Appendix D. We compare against the original LLaDA and Dream models, as well as RainbowPadding applied to these models. 

Accuracy Robustness. Table 1 shows that Void-Padding improves fixed-length robustness across both models. On LLaDA, [VOID] substantially mitigates large-block [EOS] overflow: the original model drops sharply from 76.80 at B = 64 to 28.43 at B = 512 on GSM8K, while [VOID] remains around 78. It gives the strongest mean on three of four tasks and remains competitive on HumanEval. Dream shows the same trend: the original model degrades as B grows, while [VOID] remains stable and attains the best mean score across tasks. Across all block sizes, [VOID] attains the highest four-task mean. On LLaDA, the block-size-averaged macro mean over four tasks improves by +7 .06 points over the original model and +3 .59 over Rainbow. On Dream, the corresponding block-size-averaged macro means are +17 .84 and +6 .95 , respectively. 

[EOS] Termination Efficiency. Table 2 reports the average NFE of Dream variants in Table 1 over 

B ∈ { 64 , 128 , 512 }. The original model and Rain-bowPadding use VanillaStopping, which fills the entire 512-token canvas. Since each denoising step requires one model forward pass, this setting re-quires 512 NFE under T = 512 . VoidPadding in-stead uses [EOS] Termination, which reduces NFE by 69.3% on GSM8K, 27.5% on MATH500, 41.3% on HumanEval, and 84.6% on MBPP, averaged over B ∈ { 64 , 128 , 512 }. [EOS] signal of the original model. In the main short-canvas experiment, all methods start from an initial length L0 = 64 . VoidExpansion uses a maximum length Lmax = 512 , block size B = 32 ,and tail-window size w = 16 . On LLaDA, fixed-

L0 decoding baselines are evaluated on both the original model and the [VOID] -padding checkpoint. 6 Ablation Studies 

Setup. Our ablation experiments use the same benchmarks and metrics as Section 5 and compare the original LLaDA-8B-Instruct model with the RainbowPadding and VoidPadding checkpoints. 

VoidPadding makes [EOS] Termination effective. 

Table 5 evaluates [EOS] Termination on a fixed

8 Conclusion 

We show that a root cause of [EOS] overflow in MDLMs is a training–inference role mismatch: 

[EOS] is trained as both padding and semantic ter-minator, but used only for termination at inference. VoidPadding instead uses [VOID] as padding to-kens and reserves [EOS] for semantic endings, en-abling VoidExpansion for canvas allocation and 

[EOS] Termination for early stopping. Together, our training–inference co-design improves large-block robustness, decoding efficiency, and variable-length generation. Limitations 

Many MDLM training and inference conventions are inherited from ARLMs. Our study focuses on one concrete failure mode of this transfer: [EOS] 

padding, which is largely benign under causal masking, can become fragile under bidirectional denoising because padding positions can serve as both context and denoising targets. Thus, [EOS] 

overflow should be viewed as one symptom of a broader class of ARLM-inherited assumptions that may fail in MDLMs. Other choices, including data formatting, special-token semantics, stopping rules, and batching strategies, may require similarly MDLM-specific design. Our analysis also suggests that [EOS] overflow is not primarily a model capacity failure. Instead, it emerges from the interaction between a learned length prior, bidirectional context, and standard large-block sampling rules. VoidPadding mitigates the padding–termination mismatch, but the design of effective MDLM samplers remains open. Future samplers may need to jointly consider token confi-dence, commitment timing, canvas allocation, and stopping decisions under bidirectional denoising.

A Training Configurations 

Dataset. We use the same 0.5M-example instruction-tuning dataset as the official Rainbow-Padding (Kim et al., 2025a) implementation. The data pool combines Tulu3 (Lambert et al., 2024) and SmolLM2 (Allal et al., 2024). After removing examples longer than 4096 tokens and multi-turn dialogues, we randomly sample 0.5M examples and use this identical data for all models in our experiments. 

Shared training setup. Following the official RainbowPadding training setup, we train LLaDA-8B-Instruct (Nie et al., 2026) and Dream-7B-Instruct (Ye et al., 2025) for the main experiments, and use LLaDA-8B-Base and Dream-7B-Base for additional diagnostic runs. All models are fine-tuned with LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) of rank 32 ap-plied to all linear layers, without bias terms. We use AdamW with learning rate 5 × 10 −5 and global batch size 48. LLaDA models are trained on two A800-80GB GPUs with mini-batch size 6 per GPU and 4 gradient-accumulation steps; Dream models are trained on three A800-80GB GPUs with mini-batch size 4 per GPU and 4 gradient-accumulation steps. For both models, one epoch takes approxi-mately 32–34 A800 GPU hours. Training budgets. RainbowPadding follows its official budget: one epoch for LLaDA-8B-Instruct and Dream-7B-Instruct, corresponding to 10,417 optimizer updates, and three epochs for LLaDA-8B-Base and Dream-7B-Base. All VoidPadding checkpoints use 67% of the corresponding Rain-bowPadding training budget: 7000 optimizer updates for LLaDA-8B-Instruct and Dream-7B-Instruct, and two epochs for LLaDA-8B-Base and Dream-7B-Base. The [EOS] -padding diagnostic models are trained for two epochs on LLaDA-8B-Base and Dream-7B-Base. 

67% training-budget comparison. Figures 13 and 14 compare Early RainbowPadding and Void-Padding under the same training budget. Specifi-cally, Early RainbowPadding uses 7000 optimizer updates, matching the VoidPadding training bud-get and corresponding to 67% of the official Rain-bowPadding budget for both LLaDA-8B-Instruct and Dream-7B-Instruct. All other settings are kept identical, including the training data, LoRA con-figuration, optimizer, batch size, fixed generation length, and the absence of VoidExpansion, match-ing the fixed-length generation robustness experi-ments in Table 1. Under this matched setup, Void-Padding achieves better accuracy than Early Rain-bowPadding on both models.
